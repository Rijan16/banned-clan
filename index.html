    The universe began about 13.8 billion years ago with the Big Bang, a massive explosion that set space and time into rapid expansion. Initially, it was a hot, dense mix of particles, but as it cooled, atoms formed, leading to the creation of stars, galaxies, and planets. Over billions of years, gravity shaped the universe, and our solar system emerged around 4.6 billion years ago. Today, the universe continues to expand, with mysteries like dark matter, dark energy, and the possibility of life beyond Earth still waiting to be fully understood.

    Earth Information Center

    For more than 50 years, NASA satellites have provided data on Earth's land, water, air, temperature, and climate. NASA's Earth Information Center allows visitors to see how our planet is changing in six key areas: sea level rise and coastal impacts, health and air quality, wildfires, greenhouse gases, sustainable energy, and agriculture.

    GALAXIES

    Galaxies form from immense clouds of gas and dust in the early universe, collapsing under gravity over billions of years. These clouds eventually form stars and star systems, which group into larger structures. Over time, gravity shapes galaxies into different forms like spiral, elliptical, or irregular shapes. Galaxies often contain billions of stars, planets, and dark matter, and they interact through gravitational forces, sometimes merging or passing near each other.

    Galaxy

    A galaxy is a vast system composed of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas, dust, and dark matter, all bound together by gravity. Here are some key characteristics and components of galaxies:

    • Stars: The fundamental building blocks of galaxies, which can range from small, cool red dwarfs to massive, hot blue giants.
    • Nebulae: Clouds of gas and dust where new stars are born (star-forming regions) or remnants of dead or dying stars.
    • Star Clusters: Groups of stars that are gravitationally bound together. There are two main types: open clusters (younger and less dense) and globular clusters (older and denser).
    • Dark Matter: An unseen substance that makes up a significant portion of a galaxy's mass and influences its gravitational dynamics.

    SOLAR SYSTEM

    The solar system consists of the Sun and all celestial objects bound to it by gravity, including eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, and comets. It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a rotating disk of gas and dust. The inner planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars) are rocky, while the outer planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune) are gas giants or ice giants. The Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud house many small bodies, contributing to our understanding of the solar system's evolution.
